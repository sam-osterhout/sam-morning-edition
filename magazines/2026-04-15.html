Lead Story · Foreign Policy

Ceasefire by exhaustion: U.S. and Iran reach an "in principle" extension as the blockade bites.

A two-week pause was set to expire April 22. Regional officials tell the AP it will hold — at least on paper — even as Trump publicly rejects the compromise his negotiators floated.

The 47-day war with Iran has settled into something uglier than combat and shakier than peace. According to reporting from the Associated Press picked up by Mediaite this morning, U.S. and Iranian negotiators — working through Pakistani intermediaries — have reached an "in principle" agreement to extend the current ceasefire past its April 22 expiration. The breakthrough, if it survives contact with Donald Trump's Truth Social account, would buy Washington and Tehran another window to keep shooting words instead of missiles.

That window is narrow. Trump spent Tuesday night publicly torching the very framework his team appears to have floated: a 20-year suspension of Iranian enrichment rather than a permanent ban. "I don't like what I'm reading," he told reporters, insisting Iran will "never, ever" possess a nuclear weapon. His own negotiators, meanwhile, are scheduled to sit down in Islamabad over the next 48 hours.

Below the diplomatic surface, the U.S. naval blockade of Iranian ports — announced Sunday, in full effect by Monday morning — continues to strangle what's left of Iranian oil exports. CENTCOM's Admiral Brad Cooper declared maritime superiority in the Gulf yesterday, a sentence that is either a victory lap or a dare. The IMF, noting the collapse of Gulf energy flows, cut its 2026 growth forecast for the Middle East and North Africa from 3.9% to 1.1%.

The honest read: this isn't peace. It's an armistice held together by mutual fatigue, Pakistani mediation, and the president's short attention span. If the 20-year compromise leaks further before Friday, expect Trump to blow up the deal in public and then accept a face-saving version of it by Monday. We've seen this movie.

Sebastian Gorka wants the keys to the Counterterrorism Center.

The podcast-host-turned-deputy-assistant-to-the-president is angling to run the National Counterterrorism Center — the shop that decides who counts as a terrorist.

Sebastian Gorka — the former Breitbart editor whose academic credentials and Hungarian-far-right entanglements have been litigated ad nauseam — is in the running to head the National Counterterrorism Center, according to multiple reports out of Washington overnight. The NCTC, created after 9/11 to stop another 9/11, would under Gorka become an ideological instrument pointed inward.

Gorka's entire public career has been organized around the thesis that "radical Islamic terrorism" is an existential civilizational threat, and that domestic Muslims, immigrants, and the left broadly are its fellow travelers. Giving him the NCTC isn't a promotion; it's a rewrite of the center's mission statement.

Two things to watch. First: does the Senate Intelligence Committee have anyone left — Republican or Democrat — willing to slow-walk this? Second: the NCTC directorship doesn't require confirmation for its current statutory form. Which means Gorka could be sitting in that chair by Memorial Day with no vote at all.

Hegseth's Pentagon is eating itself in the middle of a shooting war.

The Secretary of War is firing generals for refusing to purge Black and female officers from promotion lists — while U.S. forces maintain a live blockade of Iranian ports.

New reporting in the Daily Beast and New York Times documents what internal Pentagon sources are calling the worst civil-military rupture since Rumsfeld. Gen. The pattern: a Secretary of War who governs by tantrum is purging the officers who won't go along and stiff-arming the press that documents it — while 5,000 U.S. sailors sit in the Gulf enforcing a blockade whose political cover could evaporate in a week.

Rule of Law · Supreme Court

Birthright citizenship goes before the Court — and the Court seems unconvinced.

SCOTUSblog's read of Tuesday's oral argument in Trump v. Barbara: a majority of justices appeared skeptical of the executive order that tried to rewrite the Fourteenth Amendment by pen.

Donald Trump sat in the courtroom Tuesday as his own Solicitor General defended the January 2025 executive order that purported to end birthright citizenship for children of undocumented immigrants and many legal visa holders. It is the first time a sitting president has attended a Supreme Court argument in his own case. According to SCOTUSblog, it did not visibly help.

The justices who telegraphed skepticism — including two Trump appointees — kept returning to a plain-text problem: the Fourteenth Amendment says what it says. Senior U.S. District Judge John Coughenour of Seattle, whose injunction is under review, called the order "blatantly unconstitutional." Every lower court to consider the order has agreed with him.

A decision is expected by late June or early July. If the Court rules as the argument suggested, it will be the clearest judicial rebuke of a signature Trump immigration move since the 2017 travel ban rulings — and it will arrive during the presidential primary season the MAGA movement is supposed to be winning.

IMF's new 2026 growth forecast for the Middle East and North Africa — down from 3.9%.

The Gulf's economy is paying the tab for a war nobody is calling a war.

The International Monetary Fund this week slashed its 2026 growth forecast for the MENA region by more than two-thirds, citing war-related disruptions to Gulf oil and gas exports, the U.S. blockade of Iranian ports, and the rerouting of shipping around both Hormuz and the Red Sea. Airlines have pulled out. Insurance premiums on tankers have tripled. Regional travel is effectively grounded.

This is the hidden price of the "short, victorious war" framing that dominated cable in March. The shooting may have mostly stopped. The economic damage is accelerating — and it doesn't stay in the region. The IMF explicitly warned that continued disruption to oil markets could push the global economy to the edge of recession before year-end.

Election Watch · Hungary

Orbán is out. A two-year-old party just won a constitutional majority.

Péter Magyar's insurgent movement routed the Fidesz machine over the weekend. The MAGA-adjacent authoritarian coalition just lost its most successful European case study.

Sixteen years of Viktor Orbán ended at the ballot box. Péter Magyar's TISZA party — founded barely two years ago — is on track for a two-thirds parliamentary majority, strong enough to amend Hungary's constitution and roll back the "illiberal democracy" infrastructure Orbán spent a decade building. The Bulwark's Jonathan V. Last called it "MAGA International goes down in flames."

The significance goes well beyond Budapest. Orbán was the model — quoted by Tucker Carlson, hosted by CPAC, praised by Vance. His playbook of capturing courts, starving universities, and gerrymandering the media was the blueprint American conservatives pointed to as proof the authoritarian turn was stable and sustainable. It was neither.

Two early lessons. One: even stacked systems crack under the combined weight of corruption fatigue and economic stagnation. Two: the opposition that wins isn't necessarily the established center-left — it's whoever can credibly promise competence and a return to normalcy. Democrats should take notes.

The quiet nightmare for the right's ideologues: a pope who names Christian nationalism as heresy, and does it from Rome, in English, to an American audience, every week. Trump's feed is not going to fix this.

Congress inches toward ending the DHS shutdown — the longest in U.S. history.

Washington · Day 61 · April 15, 2026

Speaker Mike Johnson and Majority Leader John Thune announced Tuesday night a framework to reopen the Department of Homeland Security and end the record-long partial shutdown that began February 14. The deal, if it survives the Freedom Caucus, would fully fund DHS through September 30 while punting the underlying fight over ICE appropriations into a separate supplemental.

The shutdown's human costs have piled up: 75,000 furloughed employees, TSA attrition at airports, Coast Guard cutters tied up dockside, and a backlog of asylum and immigration proceedings that grows by roughly 4,000 cases a day.

Johnson's challenge now is arithmetic. He needs to recall members from a spring recess that wasn't supposed to end until April 21, and he needs them to vote for a deal that breaks the Freedom Caucus position that shut the department down in the first place. The question is whether House conservatives will eat a loss to unshutter DHS — or force Johnson to pass the bill with Democratic votes and take the political damage that follows.

Meanwhile: the statutory April 15 deadline for Congress to pass a budget resolution is today. It will be missed. Again.

The FISA Section 702 reauthorization vote is also scheduled for today, days before the April 20 expiration. A bipartisan amendment requiring a warrant for queries of Americans' data is expected to fail narrowly, as it has in every prior attempt.

Taken together, this is a Congress that can still legislate when the pain gets loud enough — and can't legislate the thing it's supposed to legislate, which is a budget. Normal is a long way off.
